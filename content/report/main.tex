\begin{abstract}
Machine learning compilers have emerged in recent years as a major tool for automatically optimizing the performance of deep learning models. A major component of this optimization pipeline is algebraic rewriting, where a compiler repeatedly applies graph substitutions to transform a model into an equivalent but more efficient form.

In several modern systems, these substitutions are hand-written. TASO showed that many useful substitutions can instead be \emph{discovered} automatically by enumerating candidate graph pairs and then formally verifying the valid ones using a smaller set of base operator axioms. In this paper, we study the converse question: once such substitutions have been discovered, are they already derivable as explicit compositions of the base TASO axioms inside a rewrite system? 

To answer this question, we implement an explicit graph rewrite engine over a symbolic graph IR, together with bounded meet-in-the-middle search and derivation-trace reconstruction. Evaluated on 444 non-isomorphic substitutions extracted from the TASO implementation, our system recovers $97.3\%$ of them, with most derivations requiring only a small number of rewrite steps. These results suggest that the large majority of TASO's substitutions are not independent primitive rules, but derived consequences of a comparatively small axiom basis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Machine learning compilers have become a central part of the deep learning software stack. Their role is not only to lower models to kernels, but also to transform computation graphs into equivalent forms that execute more efficiently on the target hardware. At this level, one of the most important optimization mechanisms is algebraic rewriting: replacing a subgraph with a semantically equivalent one that yields lower cost under some objective such as runtime, memory traffic, or communication volume.

A traditional way to build such optimizers is to hand-design a collection of graph substitutions. This requires substantial manual effort, scales poorly as the operator set grows, and forces the compiler writer to treat a large collection of rewrites as primitive trusted knowledge. TASO addressed part of this problem by showing that useful substitutions can instead be discovered automatically from a smaller set of base operator axioms, then formally verified before being used in optimization~\cite{jia2019taso}. This naturally leads to a further question: once TASO has discovered such substitutions, how many of them are already obtainable by repeatedly composing the original axioms inside an explicit rewrite system?

This paper studies that question directly. Rather than using SMT-based validation to prove candidate substitutions correct, we ask whether the substitutions discovered by TASO can be reconstructed as concrete rewrite derivations from TASO's base axioms. Framed this way, the issue is not whether the substitutions are valid, but whether they are already implicit in the smaller axiom set. If most of TASO's substitutions are recoverable in this sense, then a large part of the optimizer's rewrite knowledge can be understood as derived from a compact trusted core, rather than as a long list of independent primitive rules.

To investigate this, we implement an explicit graph rewrite engine over a symbolic SSA-style graph IR [maybe don't use this]. Our system supports substitution matching and application on arbitrary subgraphs, structural canonicalization for efficient deduplication, and bounded meet-in-the-middle search with derivation-trace reconstruction. We evaluate it on 444 non-isomorphic substitutions extracted from the TASO implementation, and under our search configuration recover 432 of them, or $97.3\%$. The recovered derivations are typically short, with median length 2 and maximum length 6. These results provide direct evidence that the large majority of TASO's discovered substitutions are already derivable from a comparatively small axiom basis.

\section{Motivation, and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

TASO is the most direct starting point for our work. Its goal is not merely to \emph{apply} graph substitutions efficiently, but to \emph{generate} them automatically from a smaller semantic basis~\cite{jia2019taso}. It begins from a fixed set of supported operators together with a collection of base axioms describing their equivalences. It then enumerates candidate graphs up to a chosen size bound, groups together graphs that appear equivalent under randomized testing, and uses formal verification to prove which pairs in the same candidate class are in fact sound substitutions. The result is a library of verified graph rewrites that can then be handed to an optimizer. In other words, TASO factors the optimization pipeline into two stages: an offline discovery-and-verification stage that produces substitutions, and a later rewrite stage that applies them to optimize a given model.

Subsequent work has mostly focused on the second of these stages, namely how to search more effectively through the space induced by a set of known rewrites. MetaFlow observes that restricting attention to immediately profitable rewrites can be too limiting, and therefore allows relaxed substitutions that may take locally worse intermediate steps in order to reach better final graphs~\cite{jia2019relaxed}. TENSAT instead uses equality saturation to represent many equivalent graphs simultaneously inside an e-graph, thereby reducing the phase-ordering problems of sequential rewriting~\cite{yang2021equality,willsey2021egg}. Unity extends this viewpoint to distributed training, where both algebraic rewrites and parallelization choices are represented in a unified optimization space over parallel computation graphs~\cite{unger2022unity}. These systems differ in representation and search strategy, but they share the same general perspective: the substitution set is taken as given, and the main question is how best to exploit it.

Our motivation is to look one level earlier. If the substitutions discovered by TASO are already recoverable directly as compositions of the base axioms, then the compiler pipeline can in principle be simplified. Rather than having one subsystem whose job is to discover a large set of derived substitutions and another whose job is to apply them, one could instead work directly with the rewrite system induced by the original axioms. This is attractive not only because the system becomes conceptually simpler, but also because soundness comes for free from the axioms themselves: any substitution obtained by composing sound rewrites is sound, so there is no need to separately verify each recovered rule. Even if this is not always the most practical implementation strategy, recoverability is still conceptually important. It tells us whether the discovered substitutions should be viewed as a large collection of independent primitive facts, or instead as lemmas generated by a comparatively small trusted core.

This is the perspective we take in this paper. We treat TASO's discovered substitutions as an empirical target and ask how much of that target can already be generated by the axiom basis alone. In this way, the substitution set becomes a way of measuring the expressive power of the underlying axioms, rather than only the output of a discovery procedure.

\section{Approach and Implementation}
\label{sec:approach}

\subsection{Graph IR and Substitutions}

We represent computation graphs using a symbolic graph intermediate representation. A graph is a set of bindings from tensor variables to expressions, with one defining expression per tensor. Expressions range over the TASO operators, such as \texttt{MatMul}, \texttt{Conv2D}, \texttt{Transpose}, \texttt{Concat}, and \texttt{Split}, together with non-tensor parameters such as scalars, axes, kernels, strides, padding modes, and activation modes. Inputs are represented by a distinguished \texttt{Input} expression, and outputs are made explicit using \texttt{Output} nodes.

A substitution consists of a source graph, a destination graph, and explicit correspondences between the two sides: a bijection between input tensors, a bijection between symbolic non-tensor variables, and a mapping between outputs. This is enough to represent both ordinary rewrites and rewrites where the destination output is simply one of the destination inputs, such as \texttt{transpose(transpose(x)) \(\to\) x}. Figure~\ref{fig:axiom-matmul-factor} shows one example substitution in our representation.

\subsection{Matching and Application}

Given a substitution and a target graph, we first enumerate all matches of the source graph inside the target. Matching starts from a source output tensor and a candidate target tensor, and then recursively compares the two producer expressions. Source inputs may match arbitrary target tensors, but internal source tensors must match target tensors produced by the same operator with compatible arguments. Symbolic parameters are matched in a sort-correct way, so a scalar variable can bind to a scalar literal or scalar expression, an axis variable to an axis term, and so on. A successful match records both a tensor map and a symbolic term map.

The tensor map is not required to be injective. This lets a source pattern such as \texttt{matmul(x,y)} match a target such as \texttt{matmul(x,x)}. This is important in practice, since several TASO substitutions rely on such surjective matches.

Once a match has been found, we instantiate the destination side using the symbolic bindings from the match and connect its inputs to the corresponding tensors in the target graph. If the destination output is already one of those inputs, the rewrite is just a redirection. Otherwise, we insert a fresh copy of the destination fragment into the target graph and redirect the consumers of the matched source output to the new result.

A key point is that rewriting is occurrence-local. If a tensor appears multiple times in the surrounding graph, we can rewrite only one occurrence rather than all of them at once. For example, starting from \texttt{matmul(x,x)}, the system can produce \texttt{matmul(x,transpose(transpose(x)))} by rewriting only one use of \texttt{x}. After each rewrite, we remove any now-unused bindings. This allows substitutions to apply to arbitrary subgraphs, not just to whole-graph inputs and outputs.

\subsection{Search}

For a target TASO substitution \(L \to R\), our goal is to find an explicit derivation from one side to the other. A naive forward breadth-first search quickly becomes infeasible, since the number of reachable graphs grows rapidly with depth.

Most of the axioms in our system are invertible, with only a small number of exceptions such as the split rules that delete tensors. This makes meet-in-the-middle search natural. In one orientation, we search forward from \(L\) using the full substitution set and backward from \(R\) using inverse applications of the invertible substitutions. The search stops when the two frontiers meet. If this orientation fails, we also try the reverse direction, since some substitutions are easier to recover starting from the right-hand side. In both cases we record parent pointers, so when a meeting point is found we can reconstruct the full derivation trace rather than only report reachability.

In addition to the base axioms, our search uses a small set of derived lemmas. These are not introduced as new semantic primitives, but as proof-shortening rules that make long derivations reachable under a bounded search budget. This is consistent with TASO itself, which also introduces lemma-style shortcuts during verification for some cases. In our experiments, the search is bounded by \texttt{maxDepth = 5} and \texttt{maxNumSteps = 12000}.

\subsection{Canonicalization}

During search, many generated graphs differ only by tensor naming or by redundant intermediate bindings. To avoid exploring the same state repeatedly, we canonicalize every generated graph before inserting it into the search frontier. Canonicalization performs common subexpression elimination, removes bindings not reachable from the outputs, and then renames the remaining tensors deterministically. Search therefore compares graphs by their canonical forms rather than by their original names.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluated our system on 444 non-isomorphic substitutions extracted from the TASO implementation. A substitution was counted as recovered if our bounded meet-in-the-middle search found an explicit derivation trace connecting its two sides in either orientation. Throughout these experiments, we used \texttt{maxDepth = 5} and \texttt{maxNumSteps = 12000}.

Under this search configuration, our system recovered 432 of the 444 substitutions, or $97.3\%$. This suggests that the large majority of TASO's discovered substitutions are already obtainable as explicit compositions of the underlying axiom set. In addition, the recovered derivations were typically short. Across all 432 recovered substitutions, the mean derivation length was 2.2 steps, the median was 2, and the maximum was 6.

Table~\ref{tab:deriv-stats} summarizes the distribution of derivation lengths. Most recovered substitutions required only two rewrite steps, and only a small number required more than four. This indicates that, for most of the substitution set, the axioms do not merely imply the desired rewrites in principle, but do so through relatively short derivations.

\section{Discussion, Limitations, and Next Steps}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results suggest that the large majority of TASO's discovered substitutions are already derivable from a comparatively small axiom basis. This supports the view that much of TASO's substitution library is structured rather than ad hoc: many of its rewrites behave more like derived lemmas than like unrelated primitive rules. At the same time, our results concern bounded recoverability rather than unrestricted derivability, so they should be interpreted through the lens of the search procedure used in our experiments.

\subsection{Case Study: \texttt{transpose(I) \(\to\) I}}

One instructive example is the substitution \texttt{transpose(I) \(\to\) I}. Although the result is semantically simple, it is surprisingly nontrivial to recover by explicit rewriting. Under our rewrite system, one derivation is:

\begin{quote}
\footnotesize\ttfamily
(0) transpose(I) [start]\\
(1) matmul(transpose(I), I) [inv(axiom26)]\\
(2) matmul(transpose(I), transpose(transpose(I))) [inv(axiom9)]\\
(3) transpose(matmul(transpose(I), I)) [inv(axiom16)]\\
(4) transpose(transpose(I)) [axiom26]\\
(5) I [axiom9]
\end{quote}

This case illustrates an important feature of the search space induced by the axioms. Even substitutions that appear immediate at the semantic level may lie several rewrite steps away when expressed only through primitive rules. In practice, this is one reason that a small set of derived lemmas is useful for bounded search: such lemmas do not change what is semantically derivable, but can greatly shorten the path needed to recover it.

\subsection{Next Steps}

The first next step is to analyze the remaining unrecovered substitutions in more detail. At present, they may reflect bounded-search limitations, missing lemmas, or genuine gaps in the current axiom set. Distinguishing among these possibilities would sharpen the interpretation of the current recovery rate.

A second next step is to connect derivability back to optimization performance. If most of TASO's discovered substitutions can already be generated from the base axioms, then a natural systems question is whether one can optimize graphs effectively using the rewrite system itself rather than first materializing a larger substitution library. A direct comparison between optimization driven by explicit axiom rewriting and optimization driven by TASO's precomputed substitutions would therefore be a valuable follow-up.
